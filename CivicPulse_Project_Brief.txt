CivicPulse AI - Detailed Project Brief
Prepared: June 30, 2026


1. Executive Summary

CivicPulse AI is a civic reporting, verification, tracking, and neighborhood intelligence web application.

The core idea is simple:

Citizens should be able to report local problems, the community should be able to verify what is real, administrators should be able to understand and act on the most urgent issues, and neighborhoods should be able to see whether civic life is improving or getting worse.

At its simplest level, CivicPulse is a local issue reporting system.

At its larger vision level, CivicPulse becomes a living civic intelligence layer for a city. It helps turn scattered complaints into structured public signals, community action, administrative workflows, neighborhood scores, and civic missions.

The current app is focused on Jaipur demo data and hackathon-ready functionality. It is built to be clean, structured, and expandable without becoming overcomplicated.


2. What CivicPulse AI Is

CivicPulse AI is a web app where residents can:

- Report local civic problems.
- Add location details and photos.
- Use AI to convert messy reports into structured issue summaries.
- Publish issues to a public tracker.
- Verify whether other residents saw the same issue.
- Confirm whether an issue is still unresolved or fixed.
- View issues in a list or map.
- Explore neighborhood-level civic health.
- Join community missions.

Administrators can:

- Review and manage reported issues.
- Change issue status.
- See priority queues.
- Analyze patterns across the city.
- Use AI to identify urgent issues, hotspots, duplicate clusters, and department priorities.
- Generate draft community missions.
- Create missions manually.
- Publish or delete missions.
- Review neighborhood signals.


3. Why CivicPulse AI Exists

Most civic problems fail because they are scattered.

A resident may complain on WhatsApp, another may post on social media, another may call an office, and another may simply ignore the problem because they think nothing will change. Even when problems are real, they often remain invisible, unverified, or unorganized.

CivicPulse solves this by creating a structured civic loop:

1. A citizen reports a local issue.
2. AI converts the report into a clear civic record.
3. The public can see and verify it.
4. The community can add trust signals.
5. Admins can prioritize and act.
6. Neighborhood scores update based on real activity.
7. Missions convert civic attention into action.

This creates transparency, accountability, and participation without requiring a full government integration from day one.

6. End-to-End User Journey

The main flow works like this:

1. A resident opens the Report Issue page.
2. They upload a photo of the problem.
3. They describe what happened.
4. They enter the location.
5. Google Places Autocomplete suggests matching locations.
6. When they select a place, the app captures the formatted address, latitude, and longitude.
7. The backend validates the uploaded image.
8. Gemini AI analyzes the image and text.
9. The app creates an AI-assisted draft.
10. The citizen reviews the draft.
11. The citizen can edit or cancel the draft.
12. The citizen publishes the issue.
13. The issue appears in the public tracker.
14. Other residents can verify or update the issue.
15. Admins can manage status and priority.
16. AI operations analysis can identify patterns.
17. The issue contributes to neighborhood scores.
18. Missions can be generated from civic signals.
19. The community can participate in missions.
20. Resolved issues improve civic health and responsiveness.


7. Reporting System

The reporting system is the entry point of CivicPulse.

It allows a citizen to submit a civic issue with enough context for both AI and humans to understand it.

7.1 What Citizens Submit

Citizens can submit:

- A problem description.
- A photo.
- A location.
- A landmark.
- Optional category.
- Optional urgency.
- Optional name.
- Optional contact details.

The name and contact fields are private. They are not shown publicly and are not sent to Gemini for analysis.


7.2 Image Upload

The app supports real image uploads.

It validates the actual file type and does not simply trust the file extension.

Supported formats:

- JPEG
- PNG
- WebP

Image safety and reliability rules include:

- File type validation.
- File size limits.
- Pixel/dimension limits.
- Upload path safety.
- Image preview on the frontend.

This prevents many common upload problems and keeps the system safer.


7.3 Google Places Autocomplete

The location field is improved using Google Places Autocomplete.

Instead of typing any random location text, the user gets suggestions such as:

- City Public School
- City Public Hospital
- City Mall
- Sector 3 Malviya Nagar, Jaipur

When the user selects a suggestion, Google provides:

- A formatted address.
- Latitude.
- Longitude.

This makes locations cleaner and helps the map show issues accurately.


7.4 AI Report Analysis

After the citizen submits the report, Gemini analyzes the report and returns a structured civic draft.

The AI can identify:

- Issue title.
- Short summary.
- Category.
- Severity.
- Urgency.
- Likely responsible department.
- Safety risk.
- Why the issue matters.
- Suggested next action.
- Citizen-friendly explanation.

The AI is not used as a final authority. It helps structure the report, but the citizen reviews the draft before publishing.


7.5 Draft Review

Before an issue becomes public, the citizen sees a review page.

On the review page, the citizen can:

- Read the AI-generated title and summary.
- Check category, severity, urgency, and department.
- Edit the draft if something is wrong.
- Cancel the draft.
- Publish the issue.

This prevents AI mistakes from becoming public without human review.


7.6 AI Fallback

If Gemini is temporarily unavailable, the app can still create a safe fallback draft.

This means the reporting system does not fully break if the AI provider has an outage.

However, the goal is still to use Gemini when available. The fallback exists only to protect the user flow.

11. Issue Status Lifecycle

Issues move through a simple lifecycle.

The main statuses are:

Reported:
The issue has been published but not yet strongly verified by the community.

Community Verified:
The community has confirmed that the issue is real.

Escalated:
The issue has been identified as needing administrative attention.

In Progress:
Someone is working on the issue.

Resolved:
The issue has been fixed or closed as solved.

Rejected:
The issue was invalid, inappropriate, not actionable, or a duplicate.


12. Community Verification

Community verification is one of the most important trust features.

Citizens can interact with issues using simple actions:

- I saw this too.
- Still unresolved.
- Fixed.
- Incorrect.

These signals help the system understand whether an issue is real, still active, or potentially resolved.

If three distinct people confirm "I saw this too," an issue can be promoted from Reported to Community Verified.

This makes the app more democratic and less dependent on a single report.

15. Duplicate Handling

Duplicate reports are expected in civic apps.

If many people report the same pothole or drainage problem, the system should not show all of them as separate active issues forever.

CivicPulse supports duplicate handling where admins can:

- Identify possible duplicate clusters.
- Choose which issue should remain as the original.
- Mark other issues as duplicates.
- Link duplicate issues to the original issue.

For citizens, a duplicate issue can explain why it was closed and provide a path to the original issue.

This keeps public tracking cleaner without hiding the fact that people reported the problem.

19. Civic Genome

The Civic Genome is the scoring and explanation system behind neighborhoods.

It is designed to make neighborhood condition and resident participation easier to understand.

Instead of giving one confusing score, the system separates civic condition from community activity.


20. Civic Health Score

The Civic Health Score measures overall area condition.

It reflects whether the neighborhood appears to be in good civic shape.

It is based on categories such as:

- Infrastructure.
- Cleanliness.
- Safety.
- Environment.
- Responsiveness.

Examples:

- A neighborhood with many unresolved road and drainage issues will have lower Civic Health.
- A neighborhood with resolved issues and fewer severe problems will have better Civic Health.


21. Community Power Score

The Community Power Score measures how active and helpful residents are.

It reflects community participation, not the physical condition of the area.

It is based on:

- Verifications.
- Unresolved confirmations.
- Fixed confirmations.
- Mission participation.
- Volunteer actions.

This solves a key scoring problem:

An area can have many problems but still have a strong community.

Example:

Sector 12 may have low Civic Health because of serious road issues, but high Community Power because many residents are reporting, verifying, and joining missions.


22. Score Confidence Level

Every neighborhood score includes a confidence level.

The app does not pretend every score is equally reliable.

Confidence can be:

- Low.
- Medium.
- High.

Simple confidence rule:

- Low confidence: fewer than 3 total issues/actions.
- Medium confidence: 3 to 10 total issues/actions.
- High confidence: 10 or more total issues/actions.

This prevents a new or low-activity area from looking falsely precise.


23. Score Ceilings for Serious Unresolved Issues

The Civic Health Score includes caps when serious unresolved issues exist.

This makes the score more defensible.

Rules include:

- If an area has 1 or more unresolved Critical issue, max Civic Health is 82.
- If an area has 3 or more unresolved High issues, max Civic Health is 78.
- If an area has 1 or more unresolved Critical issue older than 3 days, max Civic Health is 75.
- If an area has any unresolved issue older than 14 days, max Responsiveness is 60.

This prevents an area from showing an excellent score while serious unresolved issues remain.

The public page can explain score limits with reasons such as:

- 1 critical unresolved issue.
- 2 high-priority issues older than 3 days.
- Long unresolved issue reducing responsiveness.


24. Score Event Timeline

Neighborhood pages include a score event timeline.

This explains why scores changed.

Example events:

+3 Community Power:
Mission completed: School Road Safety Check.

-4 Civic Health:
High-severity pothole reported near school.

-2 Responsiveness:
Water leakage unresolved for more than 7 days.

+2 Safety:

This makes scoring feel transparent and real instead of fake or mysterious.


25. Gemini Score Explanation

42. Example Scenario

A resident sees a broken road near a school in Jaipur.

They open CivicPulse and report the issue with a photo.

The location field suggests the school through Google Places.

The resident selects it, and the app saves the address and coordinates.

Gemini analyzes the image and text.

It creates a draft:

- Title: Road damage near school.
- Category: Infrastructure.
- Severity: High.
- Department: Roads / Public Works.
- Safety risk: Potential accident risk for students and vehicles.

The resident reviews and publishes the issue.

The issue appears in the public tracker and map.

Other residents click "I saw this too."

After enough confirmations, the issue becomes Community Verified.

The admin sees it in the priority queue.

The Operations Agent may identify that several road issues are appearing in the same area.

The Mission Generator creates a draft mission asking residents to verify school road safety in that neighborhood.

The admin reviews and publishes the mission.

Community participation increases the neighborhood's Community Power Score.

If the road remains unresolved, Civic Health remains limited.

If it is fixed and confirmed, Civic Health improves and the score timeline explains why.
